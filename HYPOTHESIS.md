# Hypothesis And Final Assessment

## Project Question

This project investigates whether TJHSST's post-2020 admissions change altered
right-tail academic outcomes in the TJHSST geographic area.

The core question is not simply whether TJHSST's own National Merit
Semifinalist count changed. It is whether any decline at TJHSST was offset by
increases at base public schools or private schools after normalizing for class
size. If the change mostly redistributed students, the regional top-tail signal
should be relatively stable. If TJHSST's academic context and peer
concentration matter for producing exceptional PSAT outcomes, the regional
signal may fall or shift in ways that are not fully offset elsewhere.

## Working Hypothesis

The admissions change may have produced a context effect:

- Some students who would previously have attended TJHSST instead remained at
  base public schools or enrolled in private schools.
- Some students admitted under the new system may have benefited from TJHSST
  more than they would have benefited from their base school.
- The net effect is empirical. A TJHSST decline alone is not enough evidence;
  the project needs to test whether gains outside TJHSST offset it.

The main outcome for this phase is National Merit Semifinalists per 100
grade-11 students, by school, class year, pathway, and sector.

## Final Assessment From Current Public Data

The evidence supports a narrow descriptive version of the hypothesis:

- TJHSST's NMSF count and rate fall sharply in Class 2025 and remain below the
  pre-policy range in Class 2026.
- Continuously observed conventional base public schools do not show a same-year
  Class 2025 offset, then rise substantially in Class 2026.
- Combining TJHSST and base public schools shows partial local recovery by
  Class 2026, but enrollment-standardized decompositions still leave a regional
  shortfall relative to component-specific baseline rates.
- Private-school counts are complete for the focal period and rise from 47
  pre-period NMSFs to 69 post-period NMSFs, but denominator, residence,
  eligibility, and counterfactual-placement limits prevent a credible
  private-school rate or displacement-offset estimate.

The evidence does not establish the causal context-effect version. The timing
also overlaps the digital PSAT transition, COVID recovery, participation
uncertainty, enrollment shifts, and ordinary NMSF threshold/tie effects. A
causal analysis would require school-level PSAT participation and score
distributions, student/application pathway data, broader outcomes, and a
credible comparison design.

## Scope And Claim Boundary

The panel covers Classes 2019-2026 for schools in the TJHSST geographic area,
including TJHSST as its own row. Class 2025 is the first TJHSST class primarily
reflecting the post-2020 admissions process; Class 2026 is the second. Earlier
classes provide baseline context, with caveats for COVID-era disruption and
changing PSAT/NMSF conditions.

The pathway groupings are geographic analysis buckets, not observed TJHSST
admissions pathways. Private-school rows are non-public/unallocated analytical
buckets because school location alone does not identify residence, eligibility,
application status, or counterfactual public school.

Regulation 3355.16 supports the current roster treatment of non-public
applicants, but it is not the governing historical regulation for the focal
Classes 2025 and 2026. Task 9 interpretation uses Regulation 3355.14,
Regulation 3355.15, official class-specific FCPS bulletins, Board materials,
and the documented absence of archived annual Notice 3355 procedures.

Grade-11 enrollment is the outcome denominator for NMSF rates. It is not an
admissions-seat allocation denominator; any allocation analysis needs sourced
eighth-grade populations, applicant counts, offers, waitpool outcomes,
acceptances, and enrolled-student data.

## What This Project Is Not Claiming

- NMSF counts are not a complete measure of academic culture, median
  achievement, student well-being, or whether the admissions change was good or
  bad policy.
- The project does not infer academic culture from demographics.
- The project does not infer that gains at a named school came from students who
  otherwise would have attended TJHSST.
- A missing school in an incomplete source is not evidence of zero
  semifinalists.
- No numeric NMSF count, including zero, belongs in the panel without source
  URL, title, date, and source hash.

The current output is a sourced, reproducible descriptive panel and a guarded
interpretation of what public data can and cannot support.
