# Hypothesis

## Project Question

This project investigates whether TJHSST's post-2020 admissions change altered
right-tail academic outcomes in the TJHSST geographic area.

The core question is not simply whether TJHSST's own National Merit
Semifinalist count changed. It is whether any decline at TJHSST was offset by
increases at base public schools or private schools after normalizing for class
size. If the change mostly redistributed students, the regional top-tail signal
should be relatively stable. If TJHSST's academic context and peer concentration
matter for producing exceptional PSAT outcomes, the regional signal may fall or
shift in ways that are not fully offset elsewhere.

## Working Hypothesis

The admissions change may have produced a context effect:

- Some students who would previously have attended TJHSST instead remained at
  base public schools or enrolled in private schools.
- Some students admitted under the new system may have benefited from TJHSST
  more than they would have benefited from their base school.
- The net effect is empirical. A TJHSST decline alone is not enough evidence;
  the project needs to test whether gains outside TJHSST offset it.

The main outcome for this first phase is National Merit Semifinalists per 100
grade-11 students, by school, class year, pathway, and sector. If reliable PSAT
participation data become available, the project can add a modeled or direct
NMSF-per-PSAT-taker rate.

## Scope

The panel covers Classes 2019-2026 for schools in the TJHSST geographic area,
including TJHSST as its own row. Class 2025 is the first TJHSST class primarily
reflecting the post-2020 admissions process; Class 2026 is the second. Earlier
classes provide a baseline, with caveats for COVID-era disruption and changing
PSAT/NMSF cutoffs.

The pathway groupings are geographic analysis buckets, not proof of actual
TJHSST enrolled-by-pathway counts unless a source later provides those counts.
Regulation 3355.16 describes ninth-grade eligibility and evaluation, allocates
seats by public schools' 8th-grade populations, and leaves remaining seats
unallocated. Non-public applicants are considered for those unallocated seats,
so high-school NMSF buckets by pathway remain analytical geographies rather
than observed admissions-pathway counts. Private-school rows need special
handling because school location alone does not identify the student's residence
or admissions pathway.

Regulation 3355.16 is not a complete class-year implementation history. Exact
application deadlines and procedures are published through annual Notice 3355
materials, and earlier cohorts may require older regulation versions. Grade-11
enrollment is the outcome denominator for NMSF rates, not the admissions-seat
allocation denominator; any seat-allocation analysis needs sourced 8th-grade
population, applicant, offer, waitpool, acceptance, and enrolled-student data.

## What Would Support The Hypothesis

- TJHSST's normalized NMSF rate drops for affected classes.
- Nearby base public and private schools do not show offsetting normalized
  gains large enough to keep the regional rate stable.
- The pattern is concentrated in right-tail metrics such as NMSF, high SAT
  thresholds, AP 5 rates, AIME/contest outcomes, or similar measures if those
  are later added.

## What Would Weaken The Hypothesis

- TJHSST's decline is offset by gains at base public schools or private schools.
- The regional normalized NMSF rate is stable across the policy transition.
- Changes are better explained by PSAT participation, state cutoff movement,
  COVID effects, enrollment shifts, school openings, or incomplete source
  coverage.

## What This Project Is Not Claiming

- NMSF counts are not a complete measure of academic culture, median achievement,
  student well-being, or whether the admissions change was good or bad policy.
- The project should not infer academic culture from demographics.
- A missing school in an incomplete source is not evidence of zero
  semifinalists.
- No numeric NMSF count, including zero, belongs in the panel without source
  URL, title, date, and source hash.

The intended output is a sourced, reproducible panel that makes the hypothesis
testable before drawing conclusions.
