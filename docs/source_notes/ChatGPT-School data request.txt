4. Given the data reality, how can you norm NMSFs?

Here are a few realistic strategies, from “clean but coarse” to “more modeled/approximate”:

A. Normalize by school-level overall HS enrollment

This is probably the most robust thing you can do from public data:

Pull school-level 9-12 enrollment for each year from:

NCES Common Core of Data (CCD school files), or

State school profiles (often report total high school enrollment even if they don’t break out grade 11).

Use something like:

NMSFs per 1,000 high school students

as the primary normalized measure.

Pros:

Enrollment is much easier to get reliably than PSAT takers.

No guesswork about testing policies.

Cons:

It slightly penalizes schools that have a lot of 9th/10th graders relative to 11th/12th.

It ignores the fact that some schools push nearly everyone to take PSAT while others don’t.

For comparing within the same metro area and era (TJ base schools, mostly similar policies), this is often good enough.

B. Normalize by district-level PSAT participation rate + school size

If you’re willing to introduce a modeled denominator (but be very clear it’s modeled), you can do:

For each district and year, get:

11th-grade enrollment (district)

11th-grade PSAT/NMSQT takers (district)

from the SAT Suite state/district tables or those “Summary by District” reports.

Compute district participation rate:

p_district = PSAT_takers_11 / enrollment_11  (for that district, that year)

For each school in that district, approximate its number of PSAT takers as:

PSAT_takers_school ≈ p_district × (school’s 11th-grade enrollment)

If you only have total 9-12 enrollment, you can approximate 11th grade as ~¼ of 9-12, or refine that using typical grade distributions.

Then normalize:

NSMF rate (per 100 PSAT takers)
≈ NMSFs_school / PSAT_takers_school × 100

This gives you something closer to:

“Of the kids at this school who actually sat for the qualifying test, how many reached NMSF level?”

Pros:

Incorporates the fact that districts with higher PSAT participation produce more NMSFs simply because more kids sit for the test.

Allows cross-district comparisons that adjust for different “test-everyone” vs “opt-in” policies.

Cons:

Assumes uniform PSAT participation across schools within a district, which is not strictly true (magnet vs neighborhood, etc.).

Still requires school-level enrollment (ideally grade-11) plus district-level PSAT/exact participation.

If you clearly label this as “modeled PSAT takers”, it can be a very insightful metric for internal analysis even if not perfect.

C. Norm by district share instead of per-student

A simpler but cruder approach: for each district and year, compute:

share_of_district_NMSFs_school = NMSFs_school / Σ NMSFs_district

Then compare that to:

share_of_district_enrollment_school = enrollment_school / Σ enrollment_district

If a school has 10% of district students but 25% of district NMSFs, that’s interesting even if you don’t know exact PSAT counts.

Pros:

Uses relative shares instead of needing exact denominators.

Only requires district-level totals and school-level enrollment.

Cons:

Still influenced by participation patterns within district, and doesn’t give you an absolute “per X students” rate.
