Short answer: National Merit Semifinalists (NMSF) are a useful-but narrow-signal. They track the very top ≈1% of PSAT takers and are state-normed (cutoffs change yearly), so they’re great for detecting extreme-right-tail shifts at a school-but not for overall academic outcomes at the county/school level. Also, NMSF counts can swing with participation, test mode changes, migration, and policy/timing effects-so you’ll want companion metrics.

A few key facts to ground things:

NMSF status is based on the PSAT/NMSQT Selection Index, with state-specific cutoffs that move year to year; Virginia’s line has drifted across cohorts. (Compass Education Group)

TJ’s admissions overhaul happened in Dec 2020 (first cohort entering fall 2021). If you align cohorts, the first NMSF snapshot mostly reflecting that change appears with Sep 2024 (Class of 2025) / Sep 2025 (Class of 2026) announcements. (The Washington Post)

Is the PSAT/NMSF “the best” over time?

Pros

Stable construct (reasoning/reading/math), lots of historical data, clean yearly cutoff.

Very sensitive to changes in the top tail (e.g., if TJ’s policy redistributes very-high scorers among local schools, NMSF counts will show it).

Cons

Ceiling metric: tells you little about the median or broad achievement.

State-normed: a rising Virginia cutoff can depress counts even if a school’s absolute scores are flat or up.

Participation confounds: more/less PSAT test-day uptake at a school can move counts.

Small-n volatility at smaller high schools.

Publicly, rarely. Districts administer PSAT school-day but typically do not publish school-level PSAT distributions; College Board provides files to districts, not open data. You may find SAT summaries (means, %≥1200/1400) by division or in board presentations, but PSAT school-level medians usually require a FOIA/public-records request to FCPS/LCPS/APS/PWCS/FCCPS for de-identified aggregates. Thought for 11s

Mostly yes-with a few guardrails.

NMSF is excellent for tracking the extreme right tail of academic performance at a school/region because:

it’s state-normed each year (cutoff set to Virginia’s allocation), so cross-year comparisons aren’t polluted by national shifts;

it’s anchored to a well-defined test construct (PSAT/NMSQT Selection Index).

But to make it decision-grade, do these tweaks:

How to use NMSF well

Normalize by cohort size: report NMSF per 100 juniors (or per 1,000 if you prefer). Raw counts can mislead when enrollment changes.

Track participation: PSAT school-day policies and uptake matter. If the % of juniors who sat for PSAT changes, your NMSF rate will move even if ability doesn’t.

Use a relative index:  
Excellence Ratio = (school NMSF per 100 juniors) / (state NMSF per 100 juniors).

1.0 means the school produces more top scorers than the Virginia average for its size; it also auto-adjusts for year-to-year state cutoff drift.

Smooth the noise: 3-year moving average; add CIs (treat NMSF hits as rare-event/binomial). This matters for smaller schools.

Mind the tail ceiling: it tells you about the top ~1% only; it won’t reflect median culture/learning.

Pair it with 2-3 complementary “upper-tail but broader” measures

SAT upper tail: % of seniors ≥1400 (and ≥1500 if Ns allow). Less brittle than a single SI cutoff.

AP rigor/results: AP Calc BC and Physics C: % taking × % scoring 5 (per 100 juniors/seniors).

Math contest pipeline: AIME qualifiers per 1,000, AMC 12 top percentiles, USACO silver+ counts. (Great culture signal even if small-n.)

For your TJHSST policy question

To gauge effects on the sending area:

Compute each sending high school’s Excellence Ratio pre- vs post-policy (align cohorts correctly), plus SAT ≥1400% and AP BC/Phys C 5-rates.

Run a difference-in-differences against demographically similar VA schools outside TJ’s footprint to see if the right-tail density shifted in the TJ area after the lottery.

Add a participation control (PSAT/SAT participation) so you’re not picking up testing-day policy changes.

Quick takeaway

Yes: NMSF is a super useful right-tail barometer for schools/counties when normalized and paired with participation data.

No: it’s not sufficient alone to infer overall academic culture; combine it with SAT upper-tail and AP/contest pipelines for a robust view.
