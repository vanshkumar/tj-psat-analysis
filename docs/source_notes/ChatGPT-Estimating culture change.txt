Great question. You can turn this into a tractable estimation problem if you (a) define “culture” with observable proxies, and (b) use a simple peer-effects model to translate the admissions-mix shift into predicted changes in those proxies.

Here’s a pragmatic way to do it.

1) Define a measurable “academic culture index” (ACI)

Pick leading indicators that reflect TJ’s STEM/academics/learning norms—things students do, not who they are.

Examples (freshman fall → junior spring windows):

Course-taking: % placed in math ≥ Algebra II as 9th graders; % in MV Calc/Linear Algebra by 11th.

Time/effort: median weekly study hours for STEM; homework completion rates in math/CS/science cores.

Enrichment & pursuit: % doing AMC/AIME/USACO/Science Olympiad/Regeneron/Robotics; # of problem-solving sessions attended per student.

Production: % completing independent research, # papers/posters/code repos submitted per 100 students.

Persistence/fit: transfer-out rate; % requesting schedule downgrades from advanced tracks.

Teacher-rated dispositions: e.g., “self-initiates challenge problems,” “seeks feedback,” “teaches peers.” (Likert items you can combine.)

Standardize each item (z-scores) and average to form ACI (or use a first principal component). Pre-policy cohorts give you an ACI baseline and variance.

2) Classify entrants by pre-entry alignment (not demographics)

Before the policy change, label students using middle-school evidence available for all applicants/admittees:

Completed Algebra I/Geometry early, math contest participation, CS coursework, science fair participation, teacher recommendation rubrics, etc.

Create a binary or graded “STEM-alignment” score $A_i \in [0,1]$ for each student (e.g., logistic model trained on pre-policy data to predict high ACI individuals from these pre-entry features). Calibrate so that the pre-policy admits have mean $\bar A_{\text{old}}$ ~ high (whatever you measure).

Key point: this avoids stereotyping—alignment is about prior behavior/experience, not race/SES.

3) Estimate peer-effects and mapping from alignment → ACI

On historical cohorts, fit:

$$
\text{ACI}{i} = \alpha + \beta A_i + \gamma \overline{A}{\text{peers}(i)} + \delta X_i + \epsilon_i
$$

$A_i$: the student’s own alignment.

$\overline{A}_{\text{peers}(i)}$: mean alignment of classmates/clubmates/course section (you can define peers as 9th-grade math section, advisory, or club network).

$X_i$: controls (middle-school GPA, school fixed effects, cohort year).

$\beta$ captures “direct alignment,” $\gamma$ captures social/peer amplification (“linear-in-means”). In many education settings, $\gamma$ is modest but positive (think 0.05–0.25), but you’ll estimate it from TJ data.

From this you can generate a cohort-level ACI:
$$
\text{ACI}_{\text{cohort}} = \alpha + \beta \bar A + \gamma \bar A + \text{controls}
$$
≈ $\alpha + (\beta+\gamma)\bar A$ after averaging.

4) Translate the admissions-mix change into $\Delta \bar A$

Using the board’s expected composition after the merit lottery (your slides), compute how the distribution of pre-entry alignment would change.

Practical way when you don’t have microdata:

For each segment that grows under the lottery (by feeder school, region, prior course level, ED/ELL status, etc.), use earlier applicant-pool records to estimate its historical mean $A$.

Weight those means by the new shares to get $\bar A_{\text{new}}$.

The predicted culture shift is:
$$
\Delta \text{ACI} \approx (\beta+\gamma),(\bar A_{\text{new}} - \bar A_{\text{old}})
$$

If you do have applicant microdata for the years in the slide (2015/2019/2024), reweight the actual historical applicant pool to the proposed lottery shares, then recompute $\bar A$ exactly (“reweighting counterfactual”).

5) A quick toy illustration (just to see how it works)

Suppose pre-policy admits had $\bar A_{\text{old}} = 0.85$ (very high alignment).
Under the lottery, because more seats go to students without early Algebra/competitions, your reweighting yields $\bar A_{\text{new}} = 0.75$.
From historical fits, say $\beta = 0.60$ and $\gamma = 0.15$ → $\beta+\gamma = 0.75$.

Then:
$$
\Delta \text{ACI} \approx 0.75 \times (0.75-0.85) = -0.075
$$
i.e., ~0.08σ drop in the cohort’s culture index—small to moderate. If $\gamma$ were larger (stronger peer effects), the same $\Delta \bar A$ would produce a bigger shift.

6) Validate with early post-policy leading indicators

Before long-run outcomes mature, track:

9th-grade math placements, switch-down rates in the first semester.

Club sign-ups, team tryouts, contest participation rates per 100 students.

Tutoring/office-hours load and distribution across sections.

Freshman research seminar completion rates.

Transfer-out or leave-of-absence rates.
Use a difference-in-differences design with previous cohorts as controls, and (ideally) section-level fixed effects to wipe out instructor variation.

7) Put confidence bands on your estimates

Do three scenarios for $\bar A_{\text{new}}$:

Conservative (assume lottery entrants look much like historical non-admit applicants from the same feeders) → larger drop.

Middle (use weighted applicant means).

Optimistic (assume strong catch-up via summer bridge & supports) → smaller drop.

Propagate uncertainty from $\beta,\gamma$ (via bootstrap) to get error bars on $\Delta \text{ACI}$.

8) Separate mix effects from support effects

Run the model again after incorporating programmatic supports (summer bridge, math bootcamps, mentoring). Treat support exposure $S_i$ as a treatment with its own coefficient and interaction $A_i \times S_i$. You may find supports partially offset the drop in $\bar A$.

What not to do (and why)

Don’t infer culture from race/ethnicity/ED/ELL shares. Those are composition descriptors; culture is behavior and norms. Use pre-entry preparation/interest and observed actions instead.

Don’t rely only on mean GPA/SAT after admission—those are confounded by grading policies and course selection. The ACI basket above is harder to “game” and closer to the lived culture.
